# FoE: Nyari's Rise of Fame

*A spin-off following Nyari.*

Nyari has always wanted to be loved — not privately, not quietly, but by rooms full of people whose attention she commands completely. Performing is the most efficient path to that, and she is good at it in a way that is not vanity: she can read a crowd, hold a room, make a thousand strangers feel something specific at the same moment. That is a gift. She has never been sure if it is a good one.

When a manager approaches her in the elven territories with a genuine offer, it is the right words at the right moment from a person who seems to understand exactly what she is. She signs. She leaves. The contract is long, the fine print is fine, and she does not read it as carefully as she should have. She is nineteen and someone is finally offering her what she has wanted since she understood what wanting was.

**What she does not know yet:** the manager is a devil. Devils are patient. The contract she signed is binding in ways she has not begun to understand, but the binding is not the trap. The trap is that she will not want to leave.

---

- **Act I — The Life She Wanted:** The band forms around girls the devil-manager selected with the same care. Nyari and the others build something quickly — performance chemistry, late nights, the specific bond that forms between people thrown into intensity together. The crowds arrive. The adoration arrives. For a stretch of time it is everything she imagined: her name in a crowd's mouth, the specific electricity of a room that has given itself over to her, the feeling of being seen by people who came specifically to see her. She is good at this. She loves being good at this. The fact that the manager anticipated her needs perfectly, that his attention never feels like it costs him anything, that the career he built for her fits her the way a key fits a lock — she notices these things. She does not question them. She does not want to question the thing that is making her happy.

- **Act II — The Rival and the Mirror:** A second band emerges — succubi and vampires, their performances built on Echo magic. Echo magic is real and old and genuinely powerful when used honestly. What they are doing with it is something else. The fan behavior it produces looks, on the surface, like devotion: screaming crowds, obsessive dedication, people who cannot stop thinking about the performers they saw. It is not devotion. It is a compulsion. Nyari can read a room better than most, and the difference between a crowd that gives something and a crowd that has it taken is like the difference between warmth and fever. Both feel like heat. One is good.

She dislikes what the rival band is doing. She also notices, over time, that the rival band's members do not act like people who chose their methods. They act like people who are surviving. One of them — a vampire, younger than she looks, with the specific tension of someone running a clock — is visibly afraid of her own manager. Nyari files this. She does not act on it. She is not ready to acknowledge what it might mean about her own situation.

- **Act III — The Contract:** She does not discover the contract's terms through a dramatic reveal. She discovers them through accumulated small details over months — inconsistencies in the manager's explanations, a conversation with a former client of his who went silent mid-sentence when Nyari asked how long she had worked with him, the way her own access to certain booking information and financial records was always slightly more restricted than it should have been for someone co-owning her career with representation. She starts reading. Slowly, carefully, without telling anyone. She pieces the truth together from scattered clauses, cross-referenced precedents, and a conversation with one of the other band members who has been quietly suspicious longer than Nyari has.

The contract does not trap her through force. She can leave. There is no penalty, no enforcement clause, no infernal claim on her soul. The contract's terms are almost benign in their legal structure. The trap is not legal. The trap is that leaving means walking away from everything she has built — the career, the band, the crowds, the adoration, the life she wanted for years before anyone offered it to her. The devil did not need to compel her. He needed to give her exactly what she most wanted and then let her decide if freedom was worth losing it.

That is the cruelty of it. She knows, now, that she is in a contract with a being who takes ownership of performers not through infernal clauses but through dependency. As long as she wants the career more than she wants her freedom, the contract does what it was designed to do. The devil has been patient. He can afford to be. The question is whether she can afford to be patient with herself.

The weight of having brought four other people into this alongside her is not small. She does not know if they will leave with her. She does not know if she will leave at all.

- **Act IV — What They Actually Built:** Nyari starts actually learning who the other girls are — not as bandmates, not as performers, but as people with histories and contacts and their own reasons for being in this situation. This requires her to be present in a way performing never demanded: no audience, no stage, just people who are scared and need her to stop being charming and start being honest. It does not come naturally. She does it anyway.

One of the girls has a connection — a distant relationship with someone in the Angel community whose history with infernal agreements runs deep. The connection is fragile, conditional, and would require Nyari to publicly acknowledge the nature of her contract. That acknowledgment would destroy her career — the reputation she spent years building, the audience that trusts her, the future bookings that are already scheduled. There is no quiet exit available. There is only the choice between staying in a gilded contract or leaving visibly, publicly, with the full weight of what she is walking away from on display.

The band discusses it together. Not all of them decide to leave. Two of them have different calculations, different dependencies, different thresholds for what they are willing to lose. Nyari does not judge them. She understands the math better than she did before this started.

- **Act V — The Exit:** Getting free requires an arch-angel. Not because Nyari cannot leave on her own — the contract explicitly permits it — but because the devil has resources and patience and no intention of making the exit clean. The arch-angel's involvement is procedural: infernal contracts that produce performer dependency at this scale constitute a category of overreach that angels have contested since before the MOD existed. The arch-angel does not particularly care about Nyari. She is enforcing a jurisdictional precedent. The exit is a process, not a rescue.

Nyari walks away. Publicly. The announcement goes out through her own channels, in her own words, with the specific clarity she has learned in the months since she first started reading the fine print. The career ends. The crowds end. The adoration — rooms full of strangers who knew her name and nothing else — ends. She is nineteen when she signs the contract. She is older than that when she walks away from it, and older than her years in the way that comes from learning the difference between what you wanted and what wanting it cost.

The devil does not pursue her. He does not need to. His system is built on performers who stay, not performers who leave. She is a statistical outlier. He has already moved on to the next girl who wants the same thing she wanted.

- **Closing:** The band was real. The music they made was real. The industry that surrounded it was not what she thought it was, and the adoration it delivered — crowds, strangers, rooms full of people who already knew her name — turned out to be a different thing from what she actually wanted. She wanted to be known. Being watched is not the same thing.

Nyari leaves the life she built. Not because it failed her, but because she understands now that it was never going to give her what she was actually looking for. She gave up something she genuinely loved. That is the part she does not explain to people — the career was not a trap she escaped. It was a life she chose, a skill she was proud of, a version of herself she liked being. Walking away from it cost her something no contract could extract. That is how she knows it was real.

She goes to find Sersey. Not because she has nowhere else to go — because Sersey is where the real thing is, and she waited long enough to know the difference.
