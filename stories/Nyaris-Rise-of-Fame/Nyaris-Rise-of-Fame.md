# FoE: Nyari's Rise of Fame

*A spin-off following Nyari.*

Nyari has always wanted to be loved — not privately, not quietly, but by rooms full of people whose attention she commands completely. Performing is the most efficient path to that. When a manager approaches her in the elven territories with a genuine offer, it is the right words at the right moment from a person who seems to understand exactly what she is. She signs. She leaves. This story focuses less on the hunter aspect of Hyperion.

**What she does not know:** the manager is a devil. Devils shapeshift. Devils are patient. A devil who understands that the fastest path through a kitsune's social defenses is to give her what she most wants — specific, personal, seemingly genuine attention — did not need to deceive her broadly. He only needed to be exactly what she was hoping for, long enough to get a signature.

---

- **Act I — The Life She Wanted:** The band forms around girls the devil-manager selected with the same care. Nyari and the others build something quickly — performance chemistry, late nights, the specific bond that forms between people thrown into intensity together. The crowds arrive. The adoration arrives. For a stretch of time it is everything she imagined: her name in a crowd's mouth, the specific electricity of a room that has given itself over to her. She does not question how well the manager anticipated her needs, or why his attention never feels like it costs him anything.

- **Act II — The Rival and the Mirror:** A second band emerges — succubi and vampires, their performances built on Echo magic. Echo magic is real and old and genuinely powerful when used honestly; what they are doing with it is something else. The fan behavior it produces looks, on the surface, like devotion: screaming crowds, obsessive dedication, people who cannot stop thinking about the performers they saw. It is not devotion. It is a compulsion, and Nyari — who can read a room better than most — starts feeling the difference between a crowd that has genuinely given something and a crowd that has had it taken. The distinction unsettles her in ways she cannot immediately explain. She liked being adored. She is not sure she likes *this*.

- **Act III — The Contract:** She finds out what she signed. Not through a dramatic reveal but through a small detail that doesn't fit, followed by a conversation with one of the other girls who has been quietly suspicious longer than Nyari has. The contract's full terms surface. Fulfillment means becoming the devil's property — not a performer, not a talent he represents, but something he owns. The flattery that got her here was engineered for her specifically. Her ability to read people was not the defense she believed it was; it was the attack surface. The weight of having brought four other people into this alongside her is not small.

- **Act IV — What They Actually Built:** Nyari starts actually learning who the other girls are — not as bandmates, not as performers, but as people with histories and contacts and their own reasons for being in this situation. This requires her to be present in a way performing never demanded: no audience, no stage, just people who are scared and need her to stop being charming and start being honest. It does not come naturally. She does it anyway. One of the girls has a connection — a relationship with someone in the Angel community whose history with infernal agreements runs deep. The path out is not clean and it is not guaranteed, but it is real.

- **Act V — The Exit:** Getting free of a devil's contract requires power the band does not have on their own. Two sources arrive independently, for unrelated reasons. One of the bandmates has a family connection to the Angel community — an arch-angel who, once made aware of the contract's terms, intervenes not as a favor but as a matter of divine jurisdiction: infernal contracts that cross into ownership of living persons constitute a category of overreach that angels have contested since before the MOD existed. The arch-angel's involvement is cold and procedural. She does not particularly care about Nyari. The second piece is a rival devil who has been watching the contract-holder consolidate influence in a territory he considers his. Burning the contract is not kindness — it is a move in a longer game that happens to benefit the band as a side effect. The two powers work toward the same outcome for entirely different reasons and do not coordinate with each other at all. The devil loses his claim. All five of them walk out free. The arch-angel departs without waiting for thanks. The rival devil smiles in a way that reminds Nyari of the manager and she does not smile back.

- **Closing:** The band is real. The music they made is real. The industry that surrounded it was not what she thought it was, and the adoration it delivered — crowds, strangers, rooms full of people who already knew her name — turned out to be a different thing from what she actually wanted. She wanted to be known. Being watched is not the same thing. Nyari leaves the life she built, not because it failed her but because she understands now that it was never going to give her what she was actually looking for. She goes to find Sersey. Not because she has nowhere else to go — because Sersey is where the real thing is, and she waited long enough to know the difference.
