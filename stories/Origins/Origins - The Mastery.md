# Between the Wall and the World

*A standalone arc following Artem. Runs parallel to FoE: Sersey's Journey, Books VII–VIII.*

*Preceded by: Origins.md | Book VI: Artem's Emergence*

**Premise:** The Eternal Guard remanded Artem to their jurisdiction on the grounds that nothing has ever come through the Wall and MOD had no framework for what he was. What they did not anticipate is that a researcher who walked out of the Wall of Nihility with nothing but a notebook and a habit of asking questions would, in Eventide, begin reconstructing his original research from the Guard's archives — and would find that three thousand years of accumulated magical and biological knowledge had given him tools his original era never possessed. His situation is not a prison. Eventide is a home — an occupied, lived-in one — and the Guard begin as his classifiers and slowly become something else. Seravane is his colleague first and his closest friend by the end of it. Velin has decided, with the specific determination of someone who has decided, that this man's condition of not being with anyone is a correctable problem and she is going to correct it. Artem finds this more amusing than alarming, which is not the reaction she expected. The world built on his research is outside the island's edge. He is learning it from the inside out, one archived record at a time. And when word arrives of the Sleeper Incident — an Absolute Gaze of Mindscape operating inside MOD's own institutional architecture — the researcher who once created the system that now threatens a population begins developing a countermeasure. He researches for purpose, not for power.

---

- **Ch. 1–3:** The first morning. Artem wakes in the rooms the Guard assigned him — at the edge of one of Eventide's lower islands, comfortable, personal in the way of a space long enough inhabited to leave the previous resident's traces. He finds the kitchen. There is coffee, a variety that did not exist in his era, and he finds this completely acceptable. He sits down with his notebook and maps the island's geometry from the window because mapping is the reflex that survived everything else. The notebook is current-era materials and he has filled three-quarters of it since Meridian. He adds *new notebook* to a mental list that now also includes: find the archive, identify who here can be asked questions without institutional caution, and determine whether Seravane is going to stand in the doorway every time she visits or eventually come inside. She knocks before entering. She stands in the doorway both times she comes that first day. He asks if she intends to keep doing that. She says she is not sure yet. He says she can come in. She does. Her instruments are already out.

- **Ch. 4–6:** Training begins. The Guard's orientation for new jurisdictional residents is not gentle and not hostile — it is efficient. A sequence of assessments across multiple disciplines: dimensional sensing, threat-response evaluation, Source capability measurement, institutional protocol orientation. The Source gauge registers baseline human — flat, unremarkable, civilian-grade. Artem completes each session cooperatively and with the attentiveness of someone who finds the assessments genuinely interesting as data. He takes notes. In the evenings he goes back to his rooms and adds to a separate section of the notebook — observations about the Guard's internal structure, the training methodology, the specific instruments they use. His researcher's instinct is running at full capacity.

- **Ch. 7–9:** Velin. She introduces herself on the fourth training day in the way of someone who has decided to do a thing and is doing it without preamble. She is a Succubus, which she states before he could ask and with the complete ease of someone who stopped waiting for people to finish processing it before continuing the conversation. She is also, she explains while settling into a training position beside him that was not assigned to her, on a personal mission. She does not clarify the mission. She does not need to; the mission is self-evident within about forty seconds and becomes more so every time she says *Sigil of Endurance* with the specific intonation she uses for those three words. Her Endurance Sigil is visible on her forearms — deep bronze, steady, doesn't pulse — and she mentions its advantages in a context that generates a two-second silence from the other Guard members present and an expression of complete innocence from her that fills it.

Her Supreme Gaze of Enchantment — which shows each viewer their ideal eyes, different for every person — is a constant ambient presence she has no reason to deactivate. People react to her Gaze the way people always react. It is the thing she is used to. Artem does not react to it at all.

He does not know he is not reacting. The Gaze searches for a target to compute "ideal eyes" for — a person with preferences, a consciousness with a type, a subject to render an image toward. It finds nothing to target. It cycles. It recalibrates. It moves on. The effect is invisible to both of them because the failure is silent — the Gaze simply does not function where it cannot find a subject. Velin notices, gradually, that he looks at her directly without the small pause people always make when they look at her directly — the fraction of a second while their brain processes what the Gaze is showing them and decides if it is real. He just looks. She cannot remember the last time someone just looked at her.

She does not know why. She adds it to the list of things she intends to figure out about him, which is already longer than the list of things she knows.

- **Ch. 10–12:** The accident. Three weeks into training, a Guard member running a precision exercise in an adjacent corridor produces a spatial miscalculation. An Absolute Gaze activates at close range, redirected. Artem is in the corridor. The Gaze sweeps through the space he occupies. It should affect anything with a consciousness, a will, a register in the framework the Gaze is built to operate within. It finds none of those things. The Gaze completes its activation and produces no effect on the subject in its path. The Guard member stops — not because they saw Artem resist, but because the Gaze's feedback mechanism returned a null reading. No target acquired. The corridor was not empty. The Gaze says it was.

Seravane is around the corner and hears the silence. Practitioners who have worked at Absolute tier know what a Gaze completing its activation sounds like. They also know what it does not sound like — and the absence of effect is louder. She rounds the corner. Artem is standing exactly where he was, uninjured, undisturbed, noting the Guard member's expression with what appears to be academic interest. She is already writing before she has fully processed what she saw.

The formal notation goes into his file that evening: *Subject visually present. Gaze activation at Absolute tier produced zero effect. Instruments read absence in the subject's position during the activation window. Mechanism unknown. Anomaly flagged for ongoing assessment.* The Guard member who triggered the incident submits a one-paragraph report and, in the notes field, writes: *I don't think he was surprised.*

- **Ch. 13–15:** The arcane assessment. Standard evaluation protocol for jurisdictional residents includes a full arcane affinity survey — school alignment, amplitude, any forbidden-school resonance. The evaluator runs Artem's survey and then recalibrates the instrument because the output is unusual. The recalibrated instrument produces the same output. Every arcane school tested registers at maximum measurable affinity, including the six forbidden schools, which should require an entirely separate procedural escalation under standard protocol. The evaluator submits the results and an incident flag simultaneously.

The escalation reaches Seravane within the hour. She reviews it and then goes to find Artem, who is in the kitchen. She puts the survey results in front of him. He looks at them. He asks: *is this a normal survey output?* She says: no. He says: *what happens when a forbidden school registers high?* She says: normally, MOD executes the practitioner. He considers this. He says: *convenient that I am not under MOD jurisdiction.* She says this is not funny. He says he is genuinely not sure it was a joke.

The Guard formally offers him the position of Nihility expert and Wall of Nihility research consultant within two days — partly because of the survey results, partly because his theoretical knowledge of the Wall's structure is unlike anything in their archive, and partly because having him somewhere with a formal research mandate is easier to manage than having him somewhere without one. He accepts. He now has access to the archive.

- **Ch. 16–18:** The archive. Artem sits down with the pre-record holdings on the first afternoon and does not move for six hours. He is tracing the spread pattern of gazes and sigils across the eras — the river systems, the population growth, the statistical arc that no one had connected to a single origin point because no one knew there was an origin point. He cross-references his own theoretical notes (reconstructed from memory) against the MOD's modern classification system and finds the structural correspondence almost exact. His work held. Three thousand years of independent development, countless variations, whole schools of arcane theory built on top of his foundation — and every single one of them is consistent with the architecture he documented in a laboratory before the guilds existed.

Seravane comes back to find him still there, surrounded by three pages of handwritten notes and two archival documents held carefully at the edges with the technique of someone trained on materials older than these. She asks what he found. He says: *confirmation. My research framework predicted the tier structure. The MOD classification system is essentially the naming convention I was developing when they took me. They didn't know they were completing my work.* She says nothing for a moment. Then she pulls up a chair. They read in silence for a long time.

- **Ch. 19–21:** The thing with Velin continues. She has been running a campaign of proximity — showing up where he is, finding reasons to be in adjacent research rooms, adjusting the duty roster with creative reinterpretations of the assignment protocols and the specific innocent expression she reserves for situations where she has clearly done something. Artem has noted the pattern in writing. He finds it professionally interesting and personally more interesting than he expected.

The evening it shifts is ordinary. They are in the kitchen, late, the rest of the island quiet. She says something about his inability to be impressed by her — the specific frustration of someone whose most reliable tool has no effect and cannot figure out why. He asks, with genuine curiosity, what her Gaze is supposed to do. She describes it: absolute will-subsumption when it locks onto a target. The eyes it shows each person are their ideal — the exact eyes they most want to see. No two people see the same thing. No one has ever seen her real eyes without her deliberately deactivating the Gaze.

He looks at her eyes. He describes them — amber, lighter toward the center, with a particular quality of refracted light. Clinical. Precise. The way he describes everything.

She is completely still. He has just described her real eye color. He did not know he was doing something impossible. She stares at him for the length of time it takes to confirm that he is not joking, not performing, not running a counter-technique he developed in a laboratory. He has no idea what he just did. He is asking her a follow-up question about the Gaze's mechanism before she has finished processing the answer to the previous one.

She does not tell him how significant the moment is. She stores it. She will bring it up later, at a time of her choosing, when she has had time to build the right framing. She is very patient.

- **Ch. 22–24:** The recognition. Seravane has been running her own archive thread — not the same documents Artem read, but a parallel investigation through the Guard's cosmological record collection, cross-referencing formation-event accounts from multiple eras. She finds the continuity she was looking for in a sealed Second Era document: accounts of a mortal researcher whose work produced the first inheritable magical birth-traits, who was taken by the Higher Ones and sealed in the Wall of Nihility. The accounts are fragmentary and wrong in places — scholars recording what they thought they understood about something they could not have understood. The researcher's name is not in the document. Seravane does not need it.

She goes to find Artem. She puts the document on the kitchen table between them and does not say anything. He reads it all the way through. His expression does not change but the quality of the silence that comes after it does. The document confirms what he already knew but had no external corroboration for: he was not the first person the Higher Ones imprisoned. He was simply the one whose work was too large to suppress. The document describes the imprisonment as *containment of unauthorized creative architecture* — the Higher Ones' term, not the scholars'. The language is bureaucratic. The act was not.

Seravane asks: *is that you.* He says: *it doesn't say my name anywhere in there.* She says: *Artem.* He says, more quietly: *yes.*

She writes nothing for a moment. Then she asks him to tell her what actually happened — not the document's version. His. He tells her. She makes two mugs of coffee and puts one in front of him while he talks. The conversation goes long past the hour Seravane normally leaves. She does not leave. When he finishes, she sits with it for a while and then says one thing: *the world doesn't know what it was built on.* He says: *it's starting to.*

- **Ch. 25:** The question Artem has been avoiding. Two months into his research consultancy, a Guard biologist asks him a question he had not asked himself: *if you created the bridge between mutation and magic that produces Gazes, can you unmake it?* He considers this for longer than the conversation required. The answer, in strict scientific terms, is that the bridge is a fusion of two systems at a fundamental level — you cannot separate them cleanly. But you could, in theory, target the mutation component specifically. Undo the biological expression. Leave the magic intact. The Gaze would stop functioning because the body could no longer host it. It would be a cure-all — not a counter-Gaze, not a weapon, but a medical intervention that reversed the very architecture he had designed.

He does not commit to building it. He starts the theoretical work anyway, because that is what he does with interesting problems. The notebooks begin to fill with biochemical pathways, arcane interfacing diagrams, the specific junction points between physical mutation and soul-bonded magic. He consults with the Guard's medical division. He studies modern biology — three thousand years of advancement in fields that were nascent in his era — and finds that the tools he would need are surprisingly accessible. The question of whether he *should* build the serum is separate from whether he *could*. For now, he is answering the second question. The first one will wait.

- **Ch. 26–27:** Two more months pass. His position as Nihility research consultant is settled. His notebooks — plural now, since the second volume filled — contain the full theoretical framework for a Gaze-reversal compound. He has not synthesized it. The question of *should* remains unresolved. The compound would not merely counter a specific Gaze. It would undo the mutation component of any Gaze — the biological-arcane bridge he spent years building in a laboratory before the guilds existed. He created the system that gives thirty million people their abilities. He has built the theoretical off-switch.

Seravane comes to his door in the evening at a later hour than usual. She has the quality of someone who has been deciding how to say something for several hours.

She tells him: the Sleeper Incident has a Modern Era address. An Absolute Gaze of Mindscape — a bearer operating within MOD's own Gaze and Sigil Assessment Unit, partnered with a devil whose infernal contract network extends through institutional channels the Guard has been tracking — has been dream-controlling hunters, civilians, and MOD personnel for an unknown duration. The damage is not theoretical. One of the Echo Breakers — Lyra — has been dreamworked into a compliance structure. The Gaze can be contained through standard Guard intervention protocols. The dreams it planted in affected minds persist. The Guard has no instrument that reaches inside that.

She does not ask for a counter-Gaze. She asks if the theoretical framework he has been building — the Gaze-reversal compound — is ready to be tested against a live case. She has read his notebooks. She knows what the serum does. She knows the implications.

He is quiet for a moment. Then he says: *you understand what happens when MOD learns this exists. A technology that can strip any registered Gaze bearer of their ability — thirty million people, all within reach of a compound I designed in four months. They won't see a cure. They'll see control.*

She says: *I know.*

He says: *and you're asking anyway.*

She says: *a girl is dreaming someone else's dreams. She doesn't know she's not herself. We can stop the person doing it. We can't wake her up. You can.*

He opens his notebook to a blank page. He writes one line at the top. Seravane asks what it says. He says he will tell her when Lyra is awake. She says she will have his operational briefing ready in the morning. He says: *bring coffee.*

- **Closing:** The arc ends not on the serum's deployment but on its synthesis. Artem spends the evening before the Guard's operation preparing the final compound — a universal Gaze-reversal agent that targets the mutation component of the biological-arcane bridge, undoing the body's capacity to host the Gaze. It does not target a specific ability. It targets the architecture all abilities share. When administered to a Gaze bearer, the Gaze deactivates because the body can no longer express it. The magic remains — the soul-bond is intact — but the biological channel is closed. The Gaze is not countered or suppressed. It is rendered inexpressible. The bearer becomes, for the duration of the serum's effect, a non-bearer. Whether the effect is permanent or temporary depends on the dosage and the bearer's physiology. Artem has calibrated it to be reversible — a temporary suppression rather than a permanent removal — but the calibration is a choice, not a requirement. The compound can be adjusted.

The notebook is open to the page he wrote the line on. The line reads: *Gaze-reversal agent — method: biochemical disruption of the mutation-arcane interface at the Gaze-expression junction. Target: the biological bridge that permits Gaze function. Effect: Gaze deactivation via mutation reversal; magic component preserved; bearer rendered temporarily non-expressive. Can be calibrated for permanence.* Below it, in the margin, in smaller handwriting: *i built the bridge. i just built the way to close it.*

He closes the notebook. The coffee is ready in the morning. The serum is not the end of anything. It is the beginning of a problem he has been preparing for since Seravane asked the question he should have asked himself. The MOD, when they learn of its existence — a universal Gaze suppressor, developed by a being outside their jurisdiction, capable of stripping any registered bearer of their ability — will move to acquire it. Not for Mindscape. For everyone. Thirty million people, all carrying abilities that cannot currently be regulated by any institution, and a compound that changes that equation. Artem knows exactly what happens when institutions are given tools to control populations. He built the system they want to control. He has just built the tool they would use to do it. He will not let them have it.

Seravane stands in the doorway for slightly less time than usual before she comes inside. She has read the notebooks. She knows what he has and what it means. She is still here.

## Research Status — Arc's End

Artem's work during the Mastery arc is scientific, not personal. He does not develop a single Gaze or Sigil for himself. His focus is external: understanding the system he created and building a universal Gaze-reversal compound. The serum represents his first practical application of his original formula in reverse — not creating abilities, but closing the biological channel that expresses them. It is not power. It is purpose, and the weight of it is not lost on him.

| Project | Status | Notes |
|---------|--------|-------|
| Gaze-Sigil spread pattern analysis | Complete | Confirmed: original contamination followed eastern river systems; distribution matches hydrological models |
| MOD classification cross-reference | Complete | MOD tier structure maps to original theoretical framework with 92% structural correspondence |
| Universal Gaze-reversal serum | Synthesized | Targets the mutation-arcane interface at the Gaze-expression junction; suppresses any Gaze by undoing the biological bridge; calibrated to be temporary and reversible; can be recalibrated for permanence |
| Personal ability development | None initiated | Artem has not applied his formula to himself. He researches for purpose. That purpose has been external. |

## Arcane Affinity

Highest affinity: **Nihility magic, Nil space magic, Wall of Nihility magic** — residual resonance from extended exposure to the Wall's architecture. He does not practice these; the Guard has asked him not to test the Wall-adjacent affinities inside Eventide until the operational parameters are understood.

All other schools: **Maximum measurable affinity across every school tested**, including the six forbidden schools. The evaluator recalibrated the instrument. The result was the same. The Guard's position is that this is consistent with the original architect of the gaze-and-sigil system — the foundational bridge between biological mutation and arcane energy that every subsequent school of magic built on top of.

**Formal position offered:** Nihility expert and Wall of Nihility research consultant for the Eternal Guard. He accepted.

## Notes on Key Relationships

**Seravane** — anomaly specialist, the Guard's researcher in residence. Their working dynamic is two people approaching the same subject with incompatible instruments who have stopped noticing the incompatibility. She runs instruments that fail. He provides theoretical frameworks she could not construct any other way. She found the archive document that names what he is. They have been drinking coffee in the kitchen after his sessions since week three. Best friends is not a category either of them has articulated, but it describes what they are.

**Velin** — Succubus, Supreme Gaze of Enchantment, Supreme Sigil of Endurance. Her campaign is specific, creative, and patient. She deploys her Sigil's name in conversation with an intonation that suggests it, and makes no effort to pretend otherwise. Her Gaze — absolute will-subsumption, ideal-eye projection — has never failed against a target. For Artem, it produced nothing. Not resistance — absence. He saw her real eyes. He described them casually. He did not know he had done something impossible. That moment changed the nature of her interest: he is not resisting her. He is not even aware there is something to resist. She has never had to pursue someone from a position where her most reliable tool was irrelevant. She is finding the experience unexpectedly compelling. He is amused by her persistence in a way that she finds more difficult to navigate than resistance would be — she has a system for resistance; she does not have one for someone who watches her campaign with obvious entertainment and genuine curiosity.
