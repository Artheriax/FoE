# Rook

- **Race:** Arcanian
- **Age:** Unknown; appears mid-twenties

Rook does not need the job. He needs the problems.

He is Arcanian in the way that blends into any context — looks human, ordinary features, brown eyes and black hair and a build that has never been optimized for anything except sitting at a workbench for sixteen hours. He is not a hunter. He did not frame himself as one when Sersey's team assembled around him; he framed himself as someone who could build a psychic dampener capable of interfacing with a Voidling's theoretical models, which is what was needed and which he built. The distinction between "hunter" and "person who can solve the problem hunters cannot" has never seemed relevant to him.

He shows up to field situations with equipment the mission briefing did not anticipate, because he anticipated the briefing's gaps. He returns from field situations with data that becomes equipment by the following week. He is present in combat in the specific way of someone who is not primarily a combatant but has spent enough time adjacent to combat to have running theories about how to improve outcomes through mechanism rather than engagement.

### The Work

Rook approaches tech-magic the way some people approach an art: obsessively, expansively, with total indifference to the boundary between what he was asked to do and what is interesting. He was hired to build something. He built it and then kept building.

His outputs are inventions rather than spells — devices, tools, modifications, things that interface with Arcane phenomena at the material level. The psychic dampener he built for Sersey in Book II became, through multiple field iterations, a modular assembly that Lyra has tested at Void boundaries and Kaelen has used during projection sessions to regulate the return threshold. He built each iteration from field data. He asked no one's permission to keep working on it.

His specialty within tech-magic is integrative: not strongest in any single arcane school, but unusually effective at building systems that make multiple schools work together. A device that runs Frost and Terra simultaneously. A construct that reads Shock output and adjusts a shield's conductivity in response. He finds the combinations interesting in the way most mages find specialization interesting — the joint is often more interesting than either edge.

### Personality

Rook is flat and transactional in conversation because he communicates through problems rather than around them. If the conversation is about a problem, he is engaged. If the conversation is not about a problem, he is waiting for it to become one. He does not mean this unkindly; it is simply how his attention moves.

He has no other friends. This is a fact he is aware of and has not resolved. The group was assembled for a contract. The contract extended. The group kept doing things that required solutions. He kept being present to provide them. At some point the social architecture became real, which is the only condition under which it could have developed — he does not connect through intention, only through sustained proximity and shared problems. Nyari makes him laugh. He has noted this as a data point about Nyari and done nothing with it except find her company more tolerable than most.

He does not talk about having no other friends because it is not a complaint. It is a condition the current situation has resolved. He is not in a hurry to examine that too carefully.

### Goal

Build the thing that doesn't exist yet. Rook's practical goal is always the next problem that requires a solution that currently doesn't have one. His ambient goal, less examined, is to remain somewhere that keeps generating problems at the rate and variety that the Echo Breakers specifically produce. He is aware this is not a coincidence.
