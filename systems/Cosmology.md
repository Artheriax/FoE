# The Layered Cosmology

The cosmos of Fragments of Eternity is not a single reality but a stratified structure — a vertical arrangement of layers, each one a distinct stratum of existence with its own rules, its own inhabitants, and (in most cases) its own lords. The model supersedes older frameworks that described reality as a flat collection of parallel planes; those frameworks could not account for the depth-gradient of power observed across documented strata, nor for the fact that beings encountered in deeper layers cannot be reliably summoned to the surface. The Layered model accounts for both, and it is the only cosmological framework that survives institutional scrutiny.

This document covers the structure of the layered cosmos and the lord system that governs most (not all) of its inhabited strata. Specific layers are documented in their own files.

---

## The Three Axes of Depth

Reality is divided into three axes, all measured relative to Layer 0 — the material universe.

### Layer 0 — The Material Universe

Layer 0 is the universe as mortals know it: the physical cosmos, including Hyperion and every other inhabited world reachable through ordinary space. Magic exists on Layer 0, but it is imported, borrowed, or wielded by visitors from deeper layers. Humans, the dominant species of Hyperion, lack innate magical affinity; their civilization is built on technology, commerce, and the tools required to descend into Layer -1 and return with profit. MOD — the largest corporation on Hyperion — operates entirely within Layer 0, where it regulates cross-layer trade, descent licensing, artifact importation, and tournament sanctioning.

Layer 0 is not magical. It is the stratum where magic is scarcest and technology is most developed. The asymmetry is structural: because humans could not compete with deeper-layer beings on magical terms, they developed technological alternatives, and those alternatives have made Layer 0 the industrial production center of the known cosmos. A factory on Hyperion can outproduce a Crystal Cavern forge a hundred times over; the forge, however, produces goods the factory cannot make at all.

### Layer +N — Dimensions Outside the Universe

Positive layers are dimensions that exist outside the material universe entirely. They are not "above" Layer 0 in a spatial sense; they are orthogonal to it — accessible only through specific gates, rituals, or divine intervention. Luminara, Dreamveil, The Void, and Gehenna are all + layers. Each has its own rules, its own inhabitants, and its own governance structures.

Not all + layers are governed by lords. Gehenna, for example, operates under a hierarchy of demons and archdemons with no embodiment-lord system at all; its politics are infernal politics, not lordly politics. Other + layers have their own structures, some documented and some not. The Upper Lords — cosmic forces known in older texts as the Higher Ones — reside on + layers and embody positive concepts (Creation, Judgment, Balance, and so on). They are lords under the same system described below, but their embodiments are conceptual rather than elemental, and their layers operate by rules distinct from those of the negative layers.

The distinction between a + layer and a − layer is not moral. It is positional. A + layer is outside the universe; a − layer is inside it. Either can be pleasant, terrible, or both depending on the specific layer and the specific visitor.

### Layer -N — Strata Deeper Within the Universe

Negative layers are not outside the universe; they are inside it, in the same way that the deep web is inside the internet. They are ever-present but not visible from Layer 0, accessible only through specific passages — the most famous of which are the Gates of Shadows, the canonical entrances to Layer -1. Deeper layers are progressively darker, more dangerous, and more powerful. The inhabitants are stronger. The artifacts are more valuable. The lords are older, more settled into their embodiments, and less tolerant of visitors.

The messengers and champions who make their living in the deeper strata are a small and specific population, and their services are priced accordingly.

The "deeper = stronger" gradient is not arbitrary. It is a documented structural property of the layered cosmos. The same embodiment, expressed at different depths, scales with the layer — a lord on a deeper layer is more powerful than a lord of the same embodiment on a shallower one. This is why artifacts retrieved from deeper layers fetch prices orders of magnitude higher than artifacts from shallower ones: they are not merely rarer, they are objectively more powerful, forged in environments where the ambient magic runs thicker and the lords who shape it are correspondingly more formidable.

## Gates of Shadows

The Gates of Shadows are the canonical passages between Layer 0 and Layer -1. They are not the only passages — there are smaller, less stable routes used by experienced traders and desperate smugglers — but they are the only passages whose use is sanctioned, monitored, and taxed by MOD. Each Gate is a physical structure on the Layer 0 side (typically a standing stone arch in a remote location, marked by wards that identify it to those who know what they are looking for) and a metaphysical threshold on the Layer -1 side. Crossing through a Gate requires either MOD descent licensing, a lord's mark of passage, or sufficient power to ignore the institutional framework entirely. The last option is inadvisable for anyone who plans to return.

The Gates are old. Their architects are unknown. MOD maintains that they are natural dimensional weak points that have been reinforced for safe passage; older research organizations with a historical interest in cross-layer structure maintain that the Gates were built deliberately by parties whose identity has been erased from accessible record. The question is unresolved and MOD has no official position beyond the practical: the Gates work, and that is enough.

A traveler crossing through a Gate experiences the transition as a momentary darkness — a brief absence of sensation, longer than a blink, shorter than a breath — followed by the environment of the destination layer. The transition is disorienting the first few times; experienced travelers barely notice it. The Gates are bidirectional but not symmetric: the descent is smooth, the ascent is harder, and a traveler carrying heavy artifacts or wounded companions may find the return crossing significantly more taxing than the outbound one. MOD maintains medical and recovery facilities at each Gate's Layer 0 side for exactly this reason.

## The Lord System

Lords are beings who embody a specific concept, element, or aspect of existence. The Lord of Fire is not merely a powerful fire-wielder; he *is* fire, in the sense that fire on his layer is an extension of his being. The Lord of Stone is stone. The Lord of Decay is decay. This is not metaphor. It is the defining mechanic of lordship: the lord and the embodiment are linked at a level deeper than ability, and the link reshapes the bearer's body, grants them command over their element, and binds them to a lair that reflects and amplifies their embodiment.

### The Rule of Permanence

There must always be a lord for each embodiment. This is not a law imposed by any institution; it is a structural property of the cosmos, comparable to gravity. No one knows why the rule exists. No one knows what would happen if it were violated. The rule has been observed to hold across every documented layer, in every documented era. When a lord dies, the embodiment does not die with them — it transfers, instantly, to a new bearer.

The rule applies to lords on every layer, positive and negative. It does not apply to layers without lords (such as Gehenna, which is governed by a separate demonic hierarchy with no embodiment structure). It applies only to embodiments that have already been established as lord-held. Why some concepts have lords and others do not is a separate question, also unresolved, and one that the lords themselves do not seem to know the answer to.

### Succession by Combat

Lords can be challenged. Any being — mortal, magical, human, otherwise — may challenge a lord to single combat under the traditional terms. The challenge is formal; it cannot be ambush, it cannot be assassination, and it cannot be refused without significant reputational cost to the lord who refuses. If the lord is defeated, the victor has two choices:

1. **Ask for a gift.** The defeated lord grants a boon — an artifact, a treasure, a piece of knowledge, a passage right, a service. The lordship remains with the defeated lord. This is the more common outcome; most challengers fight for the reward, not the title. Lords who refuse to honor a gift-promise after defeat suffer consequences ranging from messenger-trade embargoes to intervention by deeper-layer lords who take the tradition seriously.

2. **Kill the lord and take their place.** The victor absorbs the souls of every previous bearer of the embodiment — an instant transfer — and is transformed into the new lord. The transformation is physical (the bearer's body reshapes to express the embodiment) and cognitive (the bearer gains the skills, knowledge, and experience of every previous lord). The bearer's identity, personality, and memories remain intact. The bearer retains full autonomy over how to use the inherited knowledge.

The choice between gift and usurpation is the dramatic core of the lordship system. Most challengers do not want to be lords; they want the gift. Those who do want to be lords are usually the wrong kind of person for it — ambitious, power-seeking, willing to kill for status — which means lordship turnover tends to produce worse lords, not better ones. The exceptions are rare and notable, and they tend to occur when a challenger kills a lord for reasons other than ambition (revenge, necessity, accident) and inherits an embodiment they did not actively seek.

### Succession by Lord-on-Lord Violence

When a lord is killed by another lord rather than by a challenger seeking the title, the inheritance mechanic is different. The souls of the previous lords do not enter the killer; instead, they release into the air of the layer as a layer-specific magic, which flows outward and selects a new candidate. The selection process is not random — the magic has taste, and consistently identifies a bearer "right" for the embodiment, though no scholar has successfully reverse-engineered the selection criteria. The new lord may be anywhere on the layer when the magic finds them; they may have no prior interest in lordship; they may not even know what has happened until the transformation begins.

This mechanic is the curse of lordship. A lord who kills a rival cannot inherit their embodiment and cannot control who does. The new bearer may be an ally, an enemy, a child, a stranger, a pacifist, a monster. The killer gains nothing from the kill except the removal of a rival — and the creation of an unpredictable new variable. Most lords who have considered the question conclude that lord-on-lord violence is rarely worth the risk.

The airborne-magic transition is instantaneous. There is no interim period in which a layer is without a lord. This is consistent with the Rule of Permanence and is the only reason the cosmos does not destabilize during lordly conflicts. Witnesses to the transition describe it as a visible flow — a colored mist, a heat-shimmer, a sound like distant music — that moves with apparent purpose through the layer's air until it finds its target. The flow is fast but not instantaneous; in documented cases, it has been observed for between thirty seconds and several minutes before settling on a candidate. During that interval, the layer's inhabitants watch the flow with intense interest, because whoever it settles on is about to become their new neighbor.

### The Inheritance of Self

The new lord retains their own identity. Their personality is not overwritten; their will is not subordinated to the embodiment. They can choose to stray from the path walked by previous lords — and some have. The inherited wisdom, however, is persuasive. A new Lord of Fire who absorbs ten generations of fire-lord knowledge typically finds that the accumulated experience argues strongly for continuity. Most do not dare to deviate. Those who do are usually either very wise or very foolish, and the historical record does not always make clear which.

The inheritance is not a chorus. The previous lords do not speak; their voices do not argue in the new lord's mind. What persists is their *competence* — the skills they developed, the decisions they made, the lessons they learned. The new lord knows what they knew, but the knowing is silent. A new lord can recall any technique used by any previous bearer, can recognize any artifact that any previous bearer encountered, can navigate the lair as if they had lived there for centuries — but they cannot ask the previous lords for advice, and they cannot consult the previous lords' reasoning except through the lens of the knowledge itself. The inheritance is a library, not a council.

This is the tragedy of lordship, when it is a tragedy: the new lord knows what their predecessors would have done, and they must decide whether to do the same. The weight of inherited competence is heavy. Those who carry it lightly are rare.

## Messengers

Each lord has the authority to appoint messengers — sentient beings who serve as the lord's extra hands in the world. A messenger is typically chosen from among the lord's trusted companions; historical patterns show that newly-transformed lords frequently appoint a lover or best friend as their first messenger, while older lords appoint from among their kingdom's administrative class or promote a champion whose service has impressed them. The appointment is voluntary on both sides; no one can be forced into messengerhood, and a messenger may resign if their lord permits it.

A messenger is marked with a physical mark specific to their lord — a visible sign on the body that identifies them as the lord's representative and grants them access to the lord's lair, kingdom, and resources. The mark also grants the messenger a portion of the lord's power: a messenger of the Lord of Fire can wield fire; a messenger of the Lord of Stone can shape crystal; a messenger of the Lord of the Sea can breathe water and command tides. The portion is significant — a messenger is a dangerous combatant in their own right — but it is not the lord's full power, and a messenger who presumes to challenge their own lord discovers the difference quickly. Messengers are immortal for the duration of their service, aging neither in body nor in mind. They retain their original personality, memories, and free will; they are not slaves, but servants, and a messenger who is treated as a slave frequently finds a way to make their lord regret it.

A messenger's service ends in one of three ways. The lord may release them, in which case the mark fades and the powers depart, returning the messenger to mortal status with whatever memories and skills they accumulated during service. The lord may be defeated, in which case the messenger remains in service until the new lord decides whether to keep or release them — a period that can range from moments to years, and which is frequently uncomfortable for all parties. Or the messenger may be killed, which is possible — they are immortal, not invulnerable, and a sufficiently powerful attacker can end them as surely as a sufficiently powerful attacker can end anyone. A killed messenger is gone; the lord may appoint a replacement, but the replacement does not inherit the previous messenger's memories or relationships.

Messengers serve three primary functions: advisor, representative, and merchant. As advisor, they counsel the lord on matters of the kingdom and layer. As representative, they speak with the lord's authority in dealings with other lords, with MOD, and with surface powers. As merchant, they trade the lord's goods — artifacts, minerals, knowledge, passage rights — across layers, bringing back what the lord needs or wants. The merchant function makes messengers the principal artery of cross-layer trade; most artifacts that reach Layer 0 markets pass through a messenger's hands at some point, and the mark of a messenger on a piece of goods is the closest thing to a certificate of authenticity that the cross-layer economy possesses.

## Champions

Champions are not appointed by lords. They are produced by tournaments. A champion is, in the simplest sense, a fighter who has demonstrated sufficient skill in tournament combat to be entrusted with cross-layer scouting expeditions. The champion's role is to descend (or, less commonly, ascend) to a target layer, retrieve what they were sent for — an artifact, a piece of knowledge, a creature, a sample — and return. The champion is paid by the lord, institution, or private sponsor who commissioned the expedition; the sponsor takes the retrieved goods; the champion takes the payment, the reputation, and any secondary loot they can carry.

Champions and messengers do not compete. They are distinct archetypes with distinct functions. A messenger serves a lord directly and trades on the lord's behalf, bound by the lord's mark and sustained by the lord's power. A champion is a free contractor who takes commissioned work, bound by nothing but their contract and sustained by their own skill. Some champions eventually become messengers — when a lord offers the position to a champion whose work they have admired, the champion may accept, trading independence for security and power. Some messengers were champions before their appointment. The two roles draw from the same population of skilled, ambitious, half-suicidal fighters, but they function differently in the economy and they tell different stories.

Expeditions to deeper layers carry correspondingly greater risk; the specific danger of each layer is its own subject, documented in that layer's file rather than generalized here. What is generally understood is that the champions who survive their early expeditions and accumulate successful retrievals eventually reach a level of reputation and compensation at which they no longer work for wages — either because they have retired on their earnings or because they have become valuable enough to pick their own sponsors.

## Tournaments

Tournaments are the principal social institution of Layer -1 and Layer +1. They serve four overlapping functions, any of which may be the primary purpose of a given tournament:

1. **Prize culture.** Fighters compete for valuable artifacts, minerals, and contracts offered by the hosting lord or institution. This is the most common format and the one most visible to surface audiences. The prizes range from ceremonial (a champion's token, a lord's favor) to substantial (a fire-touched weapon, a Layer -2 artifact, a messenger's mark of passage).

2. **Champion selection.** Lords — or institutions seeking to hire champions — use tournaments as auditions. A fighter who performs well may be offered a champion contract on the spot, or may be recruited into a lord's service as a long-term retainer. This is the principal pathway by which new champions enter the trade; informal recruitment outside tournament settings is rare and viewed with suspicion.

3. **Proxy war.** Two lords in dispute may each sponsor a champion and pit them against each other in tournament combat. The winning champion's lord is presumed to have demonstrated better judgment in champion-selection — and therefore better fitness to prevail in the underlying dispute. Proxy tournaments are formally non-binding, but the loser who refuses to honor the outcome suffers significant reputational damage, and a lord who refuses to honor multiple proxy outcomes quickly finds that no other lord will enter proxy arrangements with them.

4. **Entertainment.** Tournaments hosted by humans on Layer 0 — or by lords on Layer -1 and Layer +1 for primarily social purposes — function as spectator sport. These tournaments are often the largest in scale and the least consequential in stakes, but they generate significant economic activity and serve as the principal venue through which surface populations encounter layer culture. A Layer 0 city that hosts a major tournament sees an influx of magical beings, messengers, traders, and tourists that briefly transforms its economy.

Only Layer -1 and Layer +1 lords host tournaments. Lords on deeper layers do not care for the entertainment value, and lords on higher + layers consider the practice beneath their dignity. This produces a useful asymmetry: tournament culture is concentrated in the "middle" of the layered cosmos, where it serves as the principal interface between shallow and deep. A traveler who wants to understand the politics of Layer -1 attends its tournaments; a traveler who wants to understand the politics of Layer -3 must find another way in.

Tournaments hosted by humans for entertainment purposes — essentially cross-layer sports leagues — are sanctioned by MOD and represent one of the corporation's largest revenue streams. The fighters are a mix of professional champions, retired adventurers, and ambitious amateurs; the audiences are surface populations who will never descend below Layer -1 themselves but enjoy watching those who do. MOD regulates these events closely, both for safety (a magical-being fighter losing control in a Layer 0 arena is a corporate-liability event) and for profit (the broadcast rights alone fund several MOD divisions).

## Fake Lords

Not every claimed lord is a real one. The deeper layers — beyond Layer -2, where documentation thins and visitors are rare — are attractive territory for fraud. A traveler with sufficient confidence and a sufficiently remote destination can return to Layer 0 or Layer -1 claiming lordship over an embodiment that does not exist, or that exists elsewhere, or that exists but has never had a bearer. The fraud is typically perpetrated for status, for profit, or for the sheer satisfaction of having gotten away with it.

The principal tell of a fake lord is the absence of lair-affinity. A real lord's lair reflects their embodiment: the Lord of Fire's volcano, the Lord of Stone's crystal caverns, the Lord of Decay's fungal deeps, the Lord of the Sea's coastal caves. The lair responds to the lord's presence; it is shaped by them and shapes them in turn. A fake lord claims an embodiment but their claimed lair does not align with it. A self-proclaimed Lord of Greed whose lair contains no treasure, no valuables, no markers of accumulation, is plainly a fraud. A self-proclaimed Lord of Silence whose lair is loud is similarly suspect. The lair is the proof; without it, the claim is just words.

Real lords also tend to align temperamentally with their embodiments — the Lord of Fire is aggressive, the Lord of Stone is patient, the Lord of Decay is merciless, the Lord of the Sea is honorable. The inherited wisdom of previous bearers does not overwrite the new lord's nature, but it does push them toward patterns consistent with the embodiment, and over centuries the alignment becomes pronounced. A claimed lord whose personality flatly contradicts their claimed embodiment is not always a fraud (outliers exist, and a recently-installed lord has not yet had time to align), but the contradiction warrants scrutiny. A claimed Lord of Patience who cannot sit still through a conversation is probably making it up.

The presence of fake lords is treated by most institutions as a minor nuisance rather than a serious threat. MOD occasionally prosecutes particularly damaging fraud cases — typically those that have separated wealthy clients from significant sums of money — but the corporation's official position is that anyone gullible enough to be taken in by a self-proclaimed Lord of an embodiment that does not exist deserves what they get. The comedy of the phenomenon is widely appreciated; the actual harm done is usually limited. Some of the more elaborate fake-lord schemes have become part of cross-layer folklore, retold at tournaments and in messengers' quarters for the entertainment of audiences who know better.

## Cross-Layer Politics

Lords are not required to remain on their home layer. They can travel — to other layers, to other lords' territories, to Layer 0 itself. In practice, most do not, and the reasons are largely aesthetic. The transformed appearance of a lord is frightening to inhabitants of shallower layers; a Lord of Fire walking through a Layer 0 city causes panic regardless of his intentions, and a Lord of Decay visiting a Layer -1 settlement produces a similar response even among beings who should know better. Lords who wish to travel typically send their messengers in their stead, or use cross-layer projection techniques that do not require physical relocation.

Lords do visit each other, however, and the visits are not always diplomatic. Alliances, rivalries, friendships, and enmities exist between lords on the same layer and across layers. A Lord of Stone on Layer -1 may have a long-standing friendship with a lord on Layer -2 whose embodiment complements his own; they may visit each other's lairs; they may exchange messengers for diplomatic purposes; they may, in extreme cases, come to each other's aid in conflicts with third parties. Cross-layer lord-to-lord relationships are typically private, conducted through messenger intermediaries, and rarely documented in any institutionally accessible form. The lords themselves do not advertise their social lives.

The deeper a lord's layer, the less likely they are to receive visitors. Lords on the deeper layers may go centuries without seeing anyone from a shallower stratum, and the rare visits they do receive are the source of most of what is publicly known about the deeper strata. This isolation is part of why deeper-layer artifacts are so valuable — the supply chain is genuinely thin, and the few beings who retrieve them are genuinely exceptional. A champion who has successfully descended to the deeper layers and returned with goods can name their price; a champion who has done so repeatedly is, by economic definition, no longer a champion but a small economy.

The upper layers (+N) and lower layers (-N) are not in formal conflict. There is no cosmic war between Upper Lords and Lower Lords. The two axes of the layered cosmos operate, for the most part, independently. A Lower Lord who ascends to a + layer does so as a visitor, not as an invader; an Upper Lord who descends to a − layer does so as a guest, not as a conqueror. The rules of hospitality between lords of different axes are old, formal, and rarely tested. When they are tested, the consequences are significant enough that the rules are usually observed. The Upper Lords and the Lower Lords are not friends, but they are not enemies either; they are neighbors in a building with very thick walls, and they have learned over a very long time that the walls exist for a reason.
