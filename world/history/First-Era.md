# First Era

**Status:** *[Reconstructed from fragments — no complete documentary record survives]*

The First Era is the period when magic — initially a feared and unexplained phenomenon — became an accepted, integrated force in the daily life of Hyperion's developing civilizations. No formal institutions of the MOD era existed, and surviving documentation is fragmentary, but the era's cultural and demographic trajectory is visible in the archaeological record.

---

## The End of Fear

In the period immediately following the Cosmological Origins — Artem's imprisonment and the subsequent centuries of uncontrolled gaze and sigil spread — magic was treated as divine punishment, supernatural affliction, or dangerous anomaly. Communities burned suspected magic-touched individuals. Religious orders declared Gaze-bearing children cursed. Dungeon emergences were interpreted as apocalyptic signs.

The First Era marks the point at which this response became unsustainable. Gazes and sigils spread along trade routes and river systems — downstream from Artem's original laboratory contamination in the eastern territories — at a rate that made persecution logistically impossible. By the era's midpoint, an estimated 5–8% of the population in major settlement regions carried some form of inheritable magical trait. You could not burn a tenth of your neighbors and maintain a functioning society. The shift was not philosophical — it was demographic. Magic became normal because magical people became too numerous to exclude.

---

## The First Registries

The earliest documented attempts at ability categorization emerge in this era. These were not the systematic Assessment Units of later periods — they were informal traditions maintained by individual communities, temple archivists, and the first generation of what would later be called scholars. A child born with glowing eyes was recorded. A farmer who could lift a cart unaided was noted. The records were inconsistent, localized, and frequently contradicted each other — but they represent the first institutional instinct to catalogue and understand what Gazes and Sigils were, rather than simply react to them.

Three distinct registry traditions have been identified by MOD's Theoretical Research Division from First Era archaeological evidence:

- **The Eastern Ledger Tradition:** Communities along the eastern watersheds — closest to the original contamination source — maintained detailed birth registries tracking ability manifestation across generations. These ledgers are the direct ancestors of the Assessment Unit's modern registration framework, though no single ledger survives in full.

- **The Temple Codices:** Religious institutions that had initially condemned magical traits began, during the First Era, to reclassify certain Gazes as "divine marks" — a theological pivot that preserved institutional authority while accommodating demographic reality. These codices are the origin of the persistent cultural association between Holy-adjacent Gazes and divine favor, an association that MOD's secular framework has never fully displaced.

- **The Trade Route Lists:** Merchants traveling major routes compiled informal directories of ability-bearers in each settlement — not out of scholarly interest but because knowing which towns had a Gaze of Revelation bearer (who could verify goods) or a Sigil of Fortitude bearer (who could carry more) was commercially valuable. These lists are the earliest evidence of ability-bearers being valued for their practical contributions rather than feared for their differences.

## Cultural Integration

The First Era produced the foundational cultural frameworks that later eras refined into formal institutions:

- **The Practitioner-Artisan Distinction:** The first cultural recognition that combat-capable ability-bearers (hunters, in later terminology) were a distinct category from civilian ability-bearers who used their traits for trade, healing, or community service. This distinction would later be formalized in MOD's hunter vs. civilian registration tracks.

- **The Hierarchy Instinct:** The first informal recognition that not all Gazes and Sigils were equal — a Gaze of Destruction was treated differently than a Gaze of Harmony, even in communities with no formal ranking system. These informal hierarchies were the cultural substrate on which MOD's later tier system (Standard → Supreme → Absolute → Ethereal) was built.

- **The Guild Predecessors:** The earliest cooperative organizations of ability-bearers — not yet formal guilds, but groups of hunters who pooled contracts, shared hunting grounds, and established informal codes of conduct. The Solaris Vanguard's founding mythology traces its lineage to a First Era cooperative that defended a settlement during a multi-dungeon emergence, though no confirming documentation survives.

---

## The Era's End

The First Era did not end with a catastrophe. It ended with institutionalization — the transition from informal community traditions to the formal record-keeping, legal precedents, and regulatory bodies that characterize the Second Era. The exact boundary between the two is a matter of scholarly convention rather than a single documented event: the first legal ruling that cited a Gaze classification as evidence, the first treaty that acknowledged hunter jurisdiction across regional boundaries, the first document that used the word "registration" in something approximating its modern administrative sense.

By the time the Second Era's documentary record begins in earnest, the cultural shift was complete. Magic was no longer a feared aberration. It was an acknowledged fact of life, and the question had shifted from "how do we stop this?" to "how do we manage this?" — a question that has defined every institution built in the eras since.
